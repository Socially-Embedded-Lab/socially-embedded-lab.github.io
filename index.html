Mission

The Socially Embedded Lab at Ben-Gurion University studies social behavior in large-scale, online information systems. The lab investigates areas where information systems are suboptimal for people -- for example, by not meeting people's needs, goals or expectations -- and proposes new computational measures to bridge the gaps. For example, it studies the scale and scope of fake news on social media and develops methods for limiting its spread, audits large-scale algorithmic systems such as Google Search and Gmail to reduce bias and inequality, and examines online user engagement with news in order to promote better measures for quantifying it. The lab is part of the Software and Information Systems Engineering (SISE) department at BGU.

Projects

Fake news on social media
Who is sharing and who is consuming fake news on social media? What is the impact of misinformation? How can we robustly detect misinformation before it becomes widespread? What actions can we take against online misinformation and what are their ramifications? A number of research projects in the lab explore these questions using a combination of quantitative and qualitative research methods.
Bias in AI-mediated communication
Human communication is no longer "just" mediated by computers. A growing number of interactions are now fully-generated by AI with light supervision by humans through the use natural language generation models and machine learning. A prime example of that is Google's Smart Reply, which offers Gmail users three short but complete reply suggests to save them time responding to emails. And while the time-saving nature of these technologies is clear, they also raise an array of questions about the short- and long-term biases that they introduce, both in the resulting communications and the relationships behind them. We study these questions using a mixed-methods apporach that includes algorithmic audits, surveys, and experiments.
Predicting unemployment using social media
Up-to-date information about individuals' employment status is of tremendous value for a wide range of economic decisions, from hiring decisions to government policy. Yet, most current estimates are based on representative surveys that are costly, slow to produce, limited granularity, and somewhat inaccurate. These issues are even more extreme in low- and middle-income countries, where surveying infrastructure is not as developed as in high-income countries. Can information from social media contribute and complement existing estimates of labor market flows? In this line of work, we are using novel NLP techniques to accurately identify public disclosures about unemployment and develop statistical models to aggregate this information robustly over time.
Exposure to politics on social media and its impact on political behavior and attitude
As social media expands as a vector for news, it is increasingly important to understand the interplay between exposure to politics on social media and offline political behavior and attitudes. Prior work examined several aspects of media exposure and political participation, both in the general population and for specific socio-demographic groups. This line of work extends prior work by characterizing the different types of political exposure on social media and examine the causal mechanisms that influence political behavior and attitude. To that end, we are using observational data analysis and seek to combine it with survey-experiments.
Searching for science all over the world
Search engines are one of the primary sources for people to learn about science. The quality of scientific information found there is critical for students to make their first steps in acquiring knowledge and skills in science, and for non-scientists to navigate everyday life decisions using science. Despite the growing availability of information on the Web and increased internet access, inequalities persists across different cultures and languages. Do search engine work to reinforce existing inequalities or close the gaps? In this work, we focus on the quality of scientific information available on Google Search all over the world and across basic scientific concepts (e.g. mass), pressing scientific issues (e.g. global warming), and popular science concepts (e.g. electric cars). We approach this problem using an algorithmic audit of search results and through human-assessed quality by experts and volunteers.
Engagement with news
Despite the importance of news, we know relatively little about how well journalism is achieving its goals of informing citizens and fostering healthy discussion in society. In the absence of good metrics for measuring these important goals, both systems and people in newsrooms resort to shallow and incomplete measures of user engagement such as clicks, likes, or total time spent on a page. The goal of this research is to take a closer look at the different ways in which people interact with online news, and proposes new measures to help both information systems and newsrooms gain better insights about readers and content. To that end, we combine in-depth analysis of post-click user behavior on news articles, modeling of article content, and knowledge obtained using lab experiments about reading to devise new measures for quantifying reading online.
